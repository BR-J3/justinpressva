Eve. A Microchip. A Deadline.

Eve was a 12-year-old dog. She had a microchip. That microchip was registered to the Lynchburg Humane Society.

On April 10, 2026, Eve was surrendered to the Danville Area Humane Society by a family citing a change in household circumstances. The family had adopted her from the Lynchburg Humane Society in November 2025.

On April 28, 2026, Eve was euthanized. The Lynchburg Humane Society was never contacted.

DAHS Director Paulette Dean said staff scanned Eve for a microchip three times. The chip company called said the chip was not registered. Lynchburg Humane Society Director Jill Mollohan disputed that directly. Her staff located the registration in five minutes. The microchip was registered to Lynchburg as the organization on file. Five minutes. Three scans. Two weeks. One outcome.

DAHS later acknowledged that shortly after Eve was euthanized a rescue partner contacted the shelter saying they were willing to accept her. The shelter also discovered the microchip had been registered through another company connected to Lynchburg. DAHS called the outcome devastating. Lynchburg called it avoidable. Virginia State Senator Bill Stanley called it unacceptable.

Stanley launched a records review of DAHS and reviewed hundreds of pages of documentation. He posted publicly that his office was taking action. He stated he would draft legislation to stop this in Danville and ensure it does not happen again across the Commonwealth of Virginia. He endorsed a petition from advocacy group Danville Deserves Better calling on Danville City Council to review shelter practices and implement reforms. That petition gathered approximately 700 signatures. Stanley said he would work to shut down DAHS unless improvements were made.

The City of Danville responded through City Manager Ken Larking that the city was not aware of the petition, had no plans for a town hall, and that DAHS leadership had kept them informed from the outset. The city’s contract with DAHS remains in good standing.

DAHS announced procedural changes including a new intake question asking owners where an animal originally came from.

Eve’s death is documented. The procedural failure is documented. The legislative response is documented. The city’s response is documented. What is not yet documented is whether the records Senator Stanley reviewed will be preserved or destroyed.

Virginia’s records retention schedule governs how long animal shelter intake and euthanasia records must be kept. That deadline is May 28, 2026. Six days from today.

If those records are preserved the public accountability trail stays open. If they are not the documented record of what happened to Eve and every animal whose outcome followed the same process closes permanently.

Senator Stanley’s office has not issued a public statement addressing the May 28 records retention deadline as of publication.

The public record is the public record. Make of it what you will.
